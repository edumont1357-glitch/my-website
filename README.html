Democratic Party (Jacksonian)
Democrats
Limited federal government, states’ rights, and the “common man” (white male voters).
Core ideas: equal protection of the laws; an aversion to a moneyed aristocracy, exclusive privileges, and monopolies, and a predilection for the common man; majority rule; and the welfare of the community over the individual.
Core Beliefs
Core Beliefs
believed in limited federal government, broad political participation, and protecting the “common man” from concentrated economic power. They opposed a national bank and high tariffs, favored westward expansion, and emphasized states’ rights—while still asserting strong presidential authority when defending majority rule.
Economics & Money
Economics & Money
favored laissez-faire economics, low federal involvement, and a hard-money economy based on gold and silver rather than paper banknotes. They opposed the Bank of the United States, distrusted large financial institutions, and believed easy paper credit created corruption and benefited elites over ordinary workers and farmers.
Base of Support
Base of Support
drew most of their support from small farmers, frontier settlers, urban workers, and the “common man” in the South, West, and parts of the mid-Atlantic states. Their base tended to be people suspicious of big banks and concentrated wealth, favoring local control and more democratic participation.
Vision of Society
Vision of Society
envisioned a society of independent, self-sufficient farmers, artisans, and workers, where political power rested with ordinary white men rather than elites. They believed the ideal republic was a loosely governed, agrarian, and locally directed society that protected individual liberty by limiting centralized economic and political power.
Leaders & Events
Leaders & Events
leadership centered on figures like Andrew Jackson, Martin Van Buren, James K. Polk, and allies who promoted a militant defense of majority rule and limited federal economic power. Major events shaping the movement included the Election of 1828, the Bank War, the Nullification Crisis, Indian Removal, and the economic upheavals surrounding the Specie Circular and Panic of 1837. linked to Democratic financial policy.
Criticisms & Legacy
Criticisms & Legacy
were praised for expanding political participation among white men and challenging entrenched elites, but criticized for harsh Indian Removal policies, support for slavery, and financial instability. Their legacy shaped the Democratic Party as a mass-based party representing ordinary voters and advocating limited federal government and states’ rights.

Whig Party
Whigs
Economic modernization, national planning, and the “American System” of tariffs, bank, and internal improvements.
Core ideas: Whig Party centered on the American System, which called for a strong federal role in the economy through protective tariffs, a national bank, and federally funded internal improvements. Its core idea was that government should actively promote national economic growth and modernization.
Economic Modernization
Economic Modernization
championed economic modernization, promoting a diversified economy based on manufacturing, commerce, and national markets. They saw the growth of factories, banking, and trade as essential for national strength and progress.
Federal Government’s Role
Federal Government
believed the federal government should actively guide economic development, managing credit, stabilizing the currency, and funding projects that states could not effectively handle alone. They saw government intervention as essential for creating a prosperous, unified nation.
The American System
American System
promoted the American System, which combined protective tariffs, a national bank, and federal funding for roads, canals, and railroads. Its goal was to strengthen the national economy, support industry, and unify the country through infrastructure and commerce.
Social Bases
Social Bases
drew support mainly from merchants, bankers, factory owners, professionals, and prosperous farmers, especially in the Northeast and parts of the Upper South. They also attracted reform-minded Protestants who valued education, temperance, and social order.
Leaders & Events
Leaders & Events
Key Whig leaders included Henry Clay, Daniel Webster, William Henry Harrison, and Zachary Taylor, who promoted economic development and national unity. Major events included opposition to Jackson’s Bank War, the “log cabin” campaign of 1840, and efforts in Congress to manage sectional tensions over slavery.
Criticisms & Legacy
Criticisms & Legacy
were praised for promoting national development, education, and infrastructure, but criticized as elitist and too friendly to bankers and industrialists. Many former Whigs later helped form the Republican Party in the 1850s, leaving a legacy of supporting a strong federal role in economic and social progress.

Brief Comparison of the Two Parties

Democrats

  • Limited federal government; emphasis on states’ rights.
  • Suspicious of banks, paper money, and concentrated financial power.
  • Appeal to small farmers, planters, and frontier settlers.
  • Criticised Whigs as elitist and too close to bankers and industrialists.

Whigs

  • Supported a more active federal government in economic affairs.
  • Backed the national bank, tariffs, and internal improvements.
  • Appeal to merchants, professionals, manufacturers, and prosperous farmers.
  • Criticised Democrats as reckless populists who risked instability.
